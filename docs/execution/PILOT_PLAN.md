# Pilot Plan

## Purpose

This document defines the first manual-first vertical slice for the project.

The pilot is not meant to prove full profitability. It is meant to prove that the core business loop can run cleanly:

source -> draft -> blog post -> Facebook package -> publish record

## Pilot Strategy

The professional way to start is not maximum automation. It is a controlled pilot that teaches the system what matters while keeping cost, complexity, and risk low.

This pilot uses Option C:

**manual-first truth layer with thin automation and strong documentation**

## What The Pilot Must Prove

The pilot should prove:

1. the seed sources produce usable candidates,
2. the approved blog templates are practical,
3. the Facebook packaging templates are practical,
4. the review loop is manageable for one operator,
5. the publish-history chain can be preserved from day one.

## What The Pilot Is Not Trying To Prove

The pilot is not trying to prove:

1. full business profitability,
2. advanced analytics,
3. autonomous publishing,
4. paid amplification,
5. OpenClaw or role-based orchestration,
6. multi-channel scaling.

## Pilot Scope

### Niche

Food facts.

### Language

English only.

### Distribution

Facebook Page only.

### Blog Platform

WordPress.

### Publishing Mode

Scheduled queue with human approval.

## Pilot Duration

Recommended pilot duration:

**3 weeks or the first 20 approved source items, whichever comes second**

This is long enough to expose real workflow problems without turning the pilot into a full production launch.

## Recommended Pilot Volume

### Intake

1. review 30 to 40 source items,
2. shortlist 20,
3. advance 12 to 15 into drafting.

### Drafting

1. produce 10 to 12 blog drafts,
2. approve at least 8 to 10 for publication.

### Distribution

1. publish 8 to 10 blog posts,
2. create at least 1 primary Facebook package per post,
3. keep 1 backup hook or caption variant for learning.

## Pilot Weekly Structure

### Week 1: Source Qualification And Intake Discipline

Goals:

1. sample the seed sources,
2. validate source quality using the review checklist,
3. collect a strong first candidate batch,
4. reject weak or repetitive items aggressively.

### Week 2: Draft Production And Review

Goals:

1. transform shortlisted items using the approved templates,
2. test whether the template system actually fits the material,
3. tighten review rules,
4. identify common draft failure modes.

### Week 3: Publishing And Mapping

Goals:

1. publish the first clean batch,
2. derive Facebook packaging from each approved post,
3. preserve publish history and mapping data,
4. review early operational and performance signals.

## Daily Operating Routine

Recommended daily routine for the pilot:

1. review source feeds and shortlist candidates,
2. process 1 to 3 candidate items through the intake checklist,
3. draft or revise 1 to 2 blog items,
4. approve or reject pending drafts,
5. prepare Facebook packaging for approved items,
6. update the publish history and queue status.

This keeps the pilot steady without overwhelming the operator.

## Success Criteria

The pilot should be considered successful if it proves the workflow, not just if it creates pageviews.

### Operational success criteria

1. at least 5 seed sources produce usable candidates,
2. the team can move source items through the full workflow without confusion,
3. at least 8 blog posts are published cleanly,
4. every published item preserves source-to-blog-to-Facebook lineage,
5. the review loop remains manageable by one operator.

### Quality success criteria

1. the selected templates handle most accepted items without major structural rewrites,
2. weak or repetitive items are being rejected early,
3. Facebook packaging feels like a deliberate output, not an afterthought,
4. the system avoids thin or obviously weak transformations.

### Business-learning success criteria

1. there is early evidence of which sources are easiest to use,
2. there is early evidence of which template types are most productive,
3. there is early evidence of which Facebook packaging styles appear most promising.

## Failure Signals

The pilot should be treated as failing or incomplete if:

1. most seed sources produce unusable items,
2. the review loop is too heavy to sustain,
3. the templates do not fit the real source material,
4. the source-to-publish lineage breaks,
5. the project starts compensating for weak sourcing with more AI generation.

## Required Records During The Pilot

For every advanced item, preserve:

1. source domain,
2. source family,
3. source item URL,
4. selected template,
5. draft decision,
6. final blog title,
7. final blog publish record,
8. selected Facebook package,
9. Facebook publish record.

## Pilot Review At The End

At the end of the pilot, conduct a simple review:

1. which sources actually produced usable content,
2. which sources produced noise,
3. which template family was easiest to execute,
4. what slowed the workflow down,
5. what should be automated next,
6. what should still stay manual.

## Post-Pilot Recommendation

If the pilot works, the next professional move is:

1. narrow the active source list,
2. strengthen the Source Engine around the best sources,
3. formalize the draft and queue workflow in code,
4. preserve the review model,
5. automate only the parts that proved repetitive and stable.

## Definition Of Done

This pilot plan is serving its purpose when:

1. the project has a realistic first operating cycle,
2. the operator knows what to do each week,
3. the system learns from real workflow behavior before heavy implementation begins.
